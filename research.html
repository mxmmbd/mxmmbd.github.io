ETH Zurich · ForzaETH Bachelor Thesis · PBL Lab 2024
F1TENTH, ForzaETH: Autonomous Racing
Steering system design, embedded control, and structural reliability for a 1:10 autonomous race car. Feedback error under 5%, response time ~0.2 s. System integrated into the ForzaETH ROS-based autonomy stack in the context of ICRA 2024.
+
Overview

Bachelor thesis at ETH Zurich, conducted in the Project-Based Learning Lab (Department of Electrical Engineering), in collaboration with the ForzaETH F1TENTH team. Supervised by Prof. John Lygeros and Prof. Michele Magno. The project focused on steering system design, embedded control integration, and structural reliability for a 1:10 autonomous race car under competitive conditions.

The original sensor mounting used a plexiglass plate that fractured and vibrated after collisions, causing LiDAR misalignment and roughly 1.5 hours of replacement time per incident. A redesigned structural solution — developed in Siemens NX — eliminated that failure mode and restored sensor stability. The servo-actuated steering system was designed and validated with feedback error under 5% and response time approximately 0.2 s.

Embedded control was implemented using STM32, Arduino Pico, and a custom PCB, delivering real-time steering feedback within the team's ROS-based autonomy stack. The integrations contributed to the ForzaETH platform in the context of ICRA 2024. Failures across mechanical, embedded, and ROS integration layers were systematically diagnosed and resolved.

Technical Highlights
  • Redesigned plexiglass sensor mounting plate to eliminate crash-induced LiDAR misalignment and ~1.5 h replacement cycles
  • Servo-actuated steering system: feedback error under 5%, response time ~0.2 s
  • Custom test fixtures; static and dynamic load testing across slalom, figure-eight, and circular trajectories
  • All test data recorded in ROS bags for systematic comparison and validation
  • Embedded control: STM32, Arduino Pico, custom PCB — real-time steering feedback in ROS autonomy stack
  • Structural and vibration testing to evaluate chassis compliance under dynamic loads
  • Diagnosed and resolved failures across mechanical, embedded, and ROS integration layers
  • CAD work in Siemens NX

ETH Zurich · IDSC Studies on Mechatronics 2023–24
Review of Vehicle Lane Changing Models
Critical analysis of classical, probabilistic, and AI-based lane-changing models for autonomous and mixed-traffic scenarios. Supervised by Prof. Emilio Frazzoli at IDSC. Findings defended before PhD researchers and faculty.
+
Overview

First academic research experience, at the Institute for Dynamic Systems and Control (IDSC), ETH Zurich, within the Studies on Mechatronics program. Supervised by Prof. Emilio Frazzoli. The project focused on critically analyzing lane-change models for human-driven and autonomous vehicles, and assessing their relevance for mixed-traffic environments where human and autonomous agents share the road.

The work involved reviewing, categorizing, and evaluating classical rule-based, probabilistic, and AI-based lane-changing models — identifying their assumptions, behavioral fidelity, computational demands, strengths, and limitations. Findings were synthesized into a written report and defended before PhD researchers and faculty at IDSC.

Technical Highlights
  • Reviewed and categorized classical, probabilistic, and AI-based lane-changing models
  • Evaluated suitability for ADAS and fully autonomous mixed-traffic deployment
  • Identified limitations relevant to real-world heterogeneous traffic scenarios
  • Findings defended before PhD researchers and faculty at IDSC, ETH Zurich

Columbia University · ROAM Lab Research 2026
MiniBEE — Manipulator Kinematic Optimization
Optimizing the kinematic chain of the MiniBEE manipulator across 6, 7, and 8 DOF configurations to minimize singularities and self-collisions while achieving full coverage of a 20 × 20 × 20 cm target workspace.
+
Overview

Research at the ROAM Lab, Columbia University. MiniBEE is a compact robotic manipulator. The research question is identifying which DOF configuration — among 6, 7, and 8 DOF variants — achieves the fewest singularities and self-collisions while maintaining full accessibility across a 20 × 20 × 20 cm cubic workspace. This involves systematic evaluation of kinematic chain structures and joint limit configurations to maximize manipulability and dexterity across the required task space.

Work includes URDF model development, MoveIt configuration for motion planning in a ROS 2 stack, and quantitative workspace coverage and manipulability evaluation across candidate configurations.

Technical Highlights
  • Evaluating 6, 7, and 8 DOF kinematic configurations for singularity and self-collision minimization
  • Target: full coverage of 20 × 20 × 20 cm workspace
  • URDF models developed; MoveIt configured in ROS 2
  • Workspace coverage and manipulability metrics used to compare configurations quantitatively
