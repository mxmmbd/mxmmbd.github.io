ETH Zurich · ForzaETH Bachelor Thesis · PBL Lab 2024
F1TENTH, ForzaETH: Autonomous Racing
Steering system design, embedded control, and structural reliability for a 1:10 autonomous race car. Feedback error under 5%, response time ~0.2 s. Integrated in ForzaETH ROS autonomy stack for ICRA 2024.
+
Overview

Bachelor thesis at ETH Zurich (PBL Lab, Dept. of Electrical Engineering) with ForzaETH. Supervised by Prof. John Lygeros and Prof. Michele Magno. Focused on steering system design, embedded control, and structural reliability for a 1:10 autonomous race car under competitive conditions.

The original plexiglass sensor mounting plate fractured under collisions, causing LiDAR misalignment and ~1.5 h replacement time per incident. A redesigned structural solution (Siemens NX) eliminated the failure mode. Servo-actuated steering validated with feedback error under 5% and response time ~0.2 s. Embedded control via STM32, Arduino Pico, and custom PCB within the ROS autonomy stack. Contributions tied to ForzaETH's platform in the context of ICRA 2024.

Technical Highlights
  • Plexiglass mount redesigned → LiDAR stability restored; ~1.5 h replacement cycles eliminated
  • Servo steering: feedback error <5%, response time ~0.2 s
  • Custom test fixtures; static + dynamic load testing across slalom, figure-eight, circular trajectories
  • All data recorded in ROS bags for systematic validation
  • STM32, Arduino Pico, custom PCB — real-time steering feedback in ROS stack
  • Failures diagnosed across mechanical, embedded, and ROS integration layers
  • CAD in Siemens NX

ETH Zurich · IDSC Studies on Mechatronics 2023–24
Review of Vehicle Lane Changing Models
Critical analysis of classical, probabilistic, and AI-based lane-changing models for autonomous and mixed-traffic scenarios. Supervised by Prof. Emilio Frazzoli. Findings defended before PhD researchers and faculty.
+
Overview

First academic research experience at the Institute for Dynamic Systems and Control (IDSC), ETH Zurich, within Studies on Mechatronics. Supervised by Prof. Emilio Frazzoli. Critically analyzed lane-change models for human-driven and autonomous vehicles and assessed their relevance for mixed-traffic environments.

Reviewed, categorized, and evaluated classical rule-based, probabilistic, and AI-based models — identifying assumptions, behavioral fidelity, computational demands, strengths, and limitations. Findings defended before PhD researchers and faculty.

Technical Highlights
  • Reviewed and categorized classical, probabilistic, and AI-based lane-changing models
  • Evaluated suitability for ADAS and autonomous mixed-traffic deployment
  • Identified limitations relevant to real-world heterogeneous traffic
  • Findings defended before PhD researchers and faculty at IDSC

Columbia University · ROAM Lab Research 2026
MiniBEE — Manipulator Kinematic Optimization
Optimizing the MiniBEE manipulator kinematic chain across 6, 7, and 8 DOF configurations to minimize singularities and self-collisions while achieving full coverage of a 20 × 20 × 20 cm workspace.
+
Overview

Research at the ROAM Lab, Columbia University. MiniBEE is a compact robotic manipulator. The core question: which DOF configuration (6, 7, or 8) achieves fewest singularities and self-collisions while maintaining full coverage of a 20 × 20 × 20 cm cubic workspace. Involves URDF modeling, MoveIt configuration in ROS 2, and quantitative workspace and manipulability evaluation across candidate configurations.

Technical Highlights
  • Evaluating 6, 7, and 8 DOF kinematic chains for singularity and self-collision minimization
  • Target: full coverage of 20 × 20 × 20 cm workspace
  • URDF models + MoveIt configured in ROS 2
  • Workspace coverage and manipulability metrics used for quantitative comparison
